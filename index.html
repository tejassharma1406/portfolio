Investment Theses

Where I think
the opportunity is.

Four themes I believe will shape venture markets over the next decade, based on diligence work and ongoing market research.

01
AI Infrastructure and Applied AI

The durable opportunity in AI is not in model development itself, which is commoditizing quickly. It is in companies building proprietary data pipelines, domain-specific applications, and workflow integrations that create switching costs in regulated or specialized markets. Applied AI in healthcare, legal, and financial services is particularly interesting where data access and compliance expertise function as moats.

02
Digital Health Diagnostics

The shift toward continuous, data-driven monitoring is creating diagnostic categories that did not exist five years ago. Platforms that generate objective clinical data with clear reimbursement pathways and health system integration are best positioned to scale. AI-enabled diagnostics that reduce specialist burden while improving accuracy represent the most compelling near-term opportunity in this space.

03
Vertical SaaS

Horizontal SaaS markets are largely captured. The next defensible software businesses will be built for specific industries where deep workflow integration, proprietary operational data, and high switching costs create positions that horizontal competitors cannot easily replicate. The best vertical SaaS companies often look too small early and too dominant later.

04
Frontier Deep Tech and University Spinouts

University-origin companies in semiconductor equipment, synthetic biology, advanced materials, and energy technology are entering the market with IP-backed differentiation and patient capital structures. The tech transfer pipeline from research institutions remains an underexplored sourcing channel. Government investment programs are creating near-term demand tailwinds for companies that can navigate qualification and commercialization.

Deal Sourcing

How I find
early-stage opportunities.

Four approaches I use to identify and evaluate startups before they reach competitive deal processes.

01
Founder Ecosystems

Active engagement with university entrepreneurship programs, accelerators, and tech transfer offices surfaces IP-backed early-stage ventures before they reach competitive processes. University pipelines, particularly those connected to NSF I-Corps and SBIR grant recipients, are a consistent source of technically differentiated companies at the pre-seed stage.

02
Market-First Sourcing

Identifying sectors undergoing structural change through regulatory shifts, input cost changes, or technology unlocks, then working backward to find companies building for those tailwinds. Market-first sourcing produces higher-conviction deal flow in categories that generalist processes underweight and helps avoid chasing consensus themes after the signal has already been priced in.

03
Founder-Led Discovery

Conversations with early-stage founders frequently surface adjacent opportunities within the same sector. Founders tend to have sharper competitive landscape awareness than most secondary research, making founder networks a high-signal sourcing channel. The best introductions often come from founders who chose not to pursue a particular adjacent market themselves.

04
Pattern Recognition

Building mental models across sectors by reviewing pitch decks, attending demo days, and tracking cohort outcomes over time. Pattern recognition sharpens the speed and accuracy of initial screening and helps identify category-level trends before they become consensus. The goal is to develop sector-specific intuition that allows for faster and more confident initial assessments.
